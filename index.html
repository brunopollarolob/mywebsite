CHARTS: Listening to the Southern Sky

Time-Domain Radio Astronomy

Over the past decade, radio astronomy has shifted focus from static observations to time-variable phenomena. This approach has revealed a diverse range of energetic events: pulsars, magnetar bursts, and fast radio bursts (FRBs), millisecond-duration flashes detectable from cosmological distances.

FRBs, discovered in 2007, remain poorly understood. These signals originate from distant galaxies and release energies comparable to days of solar output in just milliseconds. They provide unique probes of extreme physics: matter in intense magnetic fields, relativistic plasma dynamics, and particle acceleration processes difficult to study elsewhere.

Coverage Gap in the Southern Hemisphere

Most FRB surveys operate in the Northern Hemisphere, leaving southern declinations underexplored. This includes the Galactic Center region, where magnetar populations are concentrated. South America, specifically, has reported no FRB detections to date—primarily due to limited observational infrastructure.

The CHARTS Array

The Canadian-Chilean Array for Radio Transient Studies (CHARTS) addresses this gap. It's a collaboration between Universidad de Chile, the University of Toronto's Dunlap Institute, and the Millimeter Wave Laboratory to deploy a low-frequency interferometer optimized for transient searches in the southern sky.

CHARTS operates at 300–500 MHz, a frequency range where FRBs show enhanced brightness and which remains relatively undersampled by existing facilities. The array consists of 256 dipole antennas providing a large collecting area, designed to detect low-dispersion-measure FRBs that facilitate nearby host galaxy localization. Expected detection rate: approximately 100 FRBs per year at full operation.

CHARTS dipole antenna prototype—256 of these will form the array, working together to capture radio signals from transient cosmic events

Site Location

CHARTS will be deployed at Laguna Carén, a protected park in Chile's Metropolitan Region near Santiago. The site provides low radio frequency interference and good access to southern declinations, including over 5 hours of daily Galactic Center visibility.

The F-Engine Development

Within CHARTS' signal processing architecture, the F-engine (channelization engine) performs the initial digitization and frequency channelization of antenna signals, producing data for downstream correlation and transient detection pipelines.

My thesis work addresses this component: developing a spectrometer system using demultiplexing techniques to process multiple antennas per digitizer. The approach allows a single analog-to-digital converter to handle several antenna inputs simultaneously, enabling wide-bandwidth digitization across the 256-element array without requiring individual digitizers for each antenna.

The challenge involves balancing data throughput, bandwidth requirements, and system scalability while maintaining the real-time processing necessary for transient detection in the southern sky.
