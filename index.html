CHARTS

CHARTS is a compact, wide-field interferometer designed to explore the transient radio sky in the 300–500 MHz band. Its architecture prioritizes the number of elements and instantaneous field of view over raw bandwidth, aiming to maximize the detection rate of fast radio bursts (FRBs) and other millisecond-duration transients.

CHARTS instantaneous field-of-view (blue region) showing extended coverage of the Galactic Center and southern sky.

System Architecture & Specifications

The system consists of 256 dual-polarized antennas (with one polarization currently implemented). It features a modular analog chain utilizing ultra-low noise amplifiers (ULNAs) and frequency division multiplexing (FDM) to reduce the number of physical analog-to-digital converters (ADCs). RFSoC nodes perform digitization, polyphase filter bank (PFB) channelization, and fast Fourier transform (FFT) in real-time. Downstream correlation and beamforming are handled by GPU nodes running transient search pipelines with dispersion measure (DM) dedispersion and real-time classification.

Site Location & Array Status

The chosen site is Laguna Carén, a protected park in Chile's Metropolitan Region near Santiago, which offers relatively favorable RFI conditions and has been accommodated for continuous remote operation.

Currently, the CHARTS project is in the first stages of commissioning at the Laguna Carén site. An initial 24-antenna subarray is operational, with ongoing tests to validate the complete analog and digital chain. Full deployment of the 256-antenna array is planned for late 2026.

My Contribution: F-engine & Digital Backend

The development of CHARTS has been a highly collaborative interdisciplinary effort. Analog components were primarily developed by Dr. Albert Wai Kit Lau and MSc Sebastián Manosalva, while the software for the searching nodes is being developed collaboratively by MSc Gonzalo Burgos and MSc student Juan Pablo Contreras.

My work leads the digital development of the system. This includes extensive RFSoC measurements, RFI characterizations, and specifically the design of the F-engine: the channelization engine responsible for digitizing and frequency-channelizing the massive incoming data stream.

Developing the digital spectrometer system requires implementing demultiplexing techniques to process multiple antennas per ADC. The architecture, employing a single RFSoC ADC to handle numerous inputs across adjacent frequency chains multiplexed by an FDM board, is outlined in the diagram below, using the maximum effective bandwidth of the RFSoC 4x2 of ~2.5 GHz. The specific chain frequency layout is shown in Table 3.2.

RFSoC 4x2 board utilized for the F-engine development.

Figure 3.7: FDM block diagram and frequency plan. Each FDM board combines signals from eight antennas (A-000 to A-007) into a single wideband output channel (300–2366.4 MHz) for digitization by one ADC of the RFSoC. Each RFSoC has four ADCs, therefore the number of FDM boards per RFSoC is also four (FDM-00 to FDM-03). With eight RFSoCs in total, the full CHARTS array employs 32 FDM boards (FDM-00 to FDM-31) to process all 256 antennas. The upconversion is performed using four independent LO tones (νLO1 to νLO4), each driving 4 mixers to create eight adjacent frequency chains. The resulting spectrum consists of eight contiguous chains, each 201.6 MHz wide, with guard bands of ~ 66 MHz between them to minimize interference.
