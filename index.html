The CHARTS Project: Watching the Southern Sky

The Time-Domain Radio Frontier

The last decade has established time-domain radio astronomy as one of the most dynamic frontiers of modern astrophysics. By focusing on temporal rather than spatial variability, this field has uncovered a rich variety of energetic phenomena, ranging from pulsars and magnetar bursts to the recently discovered fast radio bursts (FRBs). These millisecond-duration flashes are among the brightest events in the radio sky, yet their origins remain one of the outstanding puzzles in high-energy astrophysics.

Beyond their enigmatic nature, FRBs and related transient phenomena serve as powerful probes of extreme physical conditions, revealing how matter behaves in the presence of intense magnetic fields, relativistic outflows, and compact objects such as neutron stars. Observations of these events across multiple frequencies provide key insights into the mechanisms of coherent radio emission, particle acceleration, and plasma interactions in highly magnetized environments.

The Southern Sky Gap

Despite rapid progress, much of the sky, particularly at southern declinations, remains largely unexplored at the time-domain radio frontier. Most transient surveys have been conducted in the Northern Hemisphere, leaving limited coverage of regions such as the Galactic Center and nearby extragalactic space. Expanding observational capabilities in the south is therefore crucial to complete the global picture of the dynamic radio sky and to uncover new populations of transient sources that may be invisible to current facilities.

Enter CHARTS

The Canadian Chilean Array for Radio Transient Studies (CHARTS) project was conceived in this context. It aims to deploy a low-frequency interferometric array (300–500 MHz) in the Southern Hemisphere, combining wide sky coverage with sufficient sensitivity to detect, monitor, and help characterize fast radio bursts, magnetar flares, and other transient radio phenomena. CHARTS also seeks to strengthen the time-domain radio community within South America, where no FRB detections have yet been reported.

The F-Engine: Heart of the Digital Backend

At the heart of CHARTS lies a modular signal processing architecture that integrates scalable analog front-ends with a flexible digital backend optimized for transient searches. Within this backend, the F-engine (channelization engine) plays a central role: it digitizes and channelizes the signals from each antenna, producing frequency-domain data ready for correlation and transient search pipelines.

The development of an efficient, high-throughput F-engine is therefore a key step toward enabling CHARTS to achieve its scientific objectives, supporting real-time monitoring of the southern sky and advancing our understanding of coherent radio emission from compact astrophysical sources.

My thesis work focuses on this critical component—developing a full spectrometer system that allows a single digitizer to process multiple antennas through innovative de-multiplexing techniques, enabling wide-bandwidth digitization across CHARTS' 256-element array.

Research Journey

My path in radio astronomy has been shaped by hands-on experience with advanced digital signal processing systems, from designing spectrometers to testing transient detection capabilities.

Thesis Project: F-engine for CHARTS

2024 – Present

Developing a custom implementation for Xilinx AMD RFSoC 4x2 boards to process multiple antennas on a single digitizer through de-multiplexing. The innovation allows demodulating several antennas from a single ADC, enabling wide-bandwidth digitization of multiple 200 MHz bandwidth antennas. A single digitizer will convert 32 antenna analog signals of CHARTS' 256 elements.

Intern, Millimeter Wave Laboratory

December 2024 – Present

Developing a demux complex zooming spectrometer for CHARTS-8, advancing the signal processing capabilities for transient detection.

Guided Research (EL7850)

March 2024 – December 2024

Extended the RFSoC system to transmit arrays up to 4 GB for synthetic FRB injection into CHARTS-8. This work enabled comprehensive testing of antenna sensitivity and beam response, crucial for validating the array's transient detection capabilities.

Summer Intern, Millimeter Wave Laboratory

December 2023 – February 2024

Designed and implemented a PYNQ overlay to generate customizable waveforms (up to 64 MB) and transmit via DAC on RFSoC 4x2 board. This foundational work established the framework for synthetic signal injection used in later FRB testing.
